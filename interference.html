THE INTERFERENCE FIELD

superposition, rendered as a literal hologram

A hologram is an interference pattern — many waves superposed onto one surface. So is superposition. Here each learned feature becomes a plane-wave grating (direction = its weight vector, brightness = its norm), and the model's whole feature set is superposed into one fringe field — the optical twin of packing many features into one space. As you change sparsity and the features fan out, the fringes reorganize. This is the smear, made of light.

How the hologram is made. The model is the 2-dimensional superposition autoencoder (5→2 generalized to N→2); after each training step its feature vectors point in directions across the plane. We treat each feature f as a wave aᵥcos(k·(Wᵥ·r) + φ) and sum them — exactly the superposition of wavefronts that records a hologram. The bright/dark fringes are where features reinforce or cancel: the interference the network accepts in order to store more features than it has dimensions. Dense → few gratings, simple fringes; sparse → many gratings overlapping, a rich moiré. A from-scratch JS reimplementation built on Anthropic's Toy Models of Superposition (Elhage…Olah 2022, MIT) — math re-derived, trained live; the phenomenon is theirs.
